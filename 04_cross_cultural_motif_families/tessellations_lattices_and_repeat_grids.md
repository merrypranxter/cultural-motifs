# Tessellations, Lattices, and Repeat Grids

## Purpose

This file compares repeating geometric and grid-based motif systems across traditions. The goal is to identify shared formal logic (tiling, symmetry, repeat behavior) while preserving the differences in construction method, material context, and cultural specificity.

---

## Why these forms are grouped together

Repeating geometric patterns appear across many traditions. They share:

- modular unit logic (a base unit that tiles to fill a surface)
- defined symmetry operations (rotation, reflection, glide reflection, translation)
- surface-covering behavior (the pattern extends to fill available space)

They are grouped here because:
- generative art systems rely on repeat logic
- prompt language often conflates different geometric traditions
- understanding the construction method helps produce more accurate outputs

---

## Shared visual logic

- **Repeat unit** — all traditions use some form of base unit that can be replicated to cover a surface
- **Symmetry operations** — reflection, rotation, translation, and glide reflection appear across traditions, but preferred symmetry types differ
- **Edge matching** — the repeat unit must connect to its neighbors; how this connection works varies by tradition and technique
- **Density** — geometric repeats range from sparse (motifs separated by open ground) to dense (no background visible)
- **Figure-ground behavior** — some traditions emphasize the motif on a ground; others create interlocking fields where figure and ground are ambiguous

## Major cultural variants

### Islamic geometric tessellations
- **Construction:** built on precise geometric grids using compass-and-straightedge methods. Star-polygon systems (6-, 8-, 10-, 12-fold and higher) generate complex, mathematically exact patterns.
- **Preferred symmetries:** high-order rotational symmetry (often 6-fold or 10-fold). Many patterns have both rotational and reflective symmetry.
- **Material:** cut-tile mosaic (zellige), painted tile, carved stucco, inlaid wood, manuscript illumination.
- **Behavior:** patterns are typically designed to extend indefinitely — no inherent center or edge. The viewer sees a window into an unbounded field.
- **Key formal trait:** interlocking strapwork (girih lines) weaving over and under, creating a layered visual effect.
- **Distinction:** Islamic tessellations are not "just geometry." They are constructed using specific historical methods and follow tradition-specific conventions in star type, color, and material.

### Mesoamerican stepped frets and grids
- **Construction:** angular, rectilinear step patterns. Based on 90-degree turns and right-angle spirals. Not typically compass-and-straightedge derived.
- **Preferred symmetries:** bilateral and translational. Rotational symmetry is less common than in Islamic patterns.
- **Material:** carved stone, painted stucco, codex painting, ceramic surface decoration.
- **Behavior:** often used as borders or contained panels rather than infinite-field patterns.
- **Key formal trait:** the stepped fret (xicalcoliuhqui) is angular where Islamic tessellations are often curvilinear or star-based. The visual rhythm is staccato, not flowing.
- **Distinction:** Mesoamerican geometry is not simpler than Islamic geometry — it follows different construction logic driven by different material and compositional contexts.

### Japanese textile repeat patterns
- **Construction:** modular repeat units designed for woven, dyed, or printed fabric. Constrained by loom structure and dyeing technique.
- **Common patterns:** asanoha (hemp leaf, hexagonal), seigaiha (blue ocean waves, overlapping arcs), shippo (seven treasures, interlocking circles), yagasuri (arrow feathers, chevron).
- **Preferred symmetries:** translational repeat with bilateral symmetry within the unit. Hexagonal tiling (asanoha) is a notable exception using 6-fold structure.
- **Material:** silk, cotton, and paper; stencil-dyed, woven, or block-printed.
- **Behavior:** modular and tileable, designed for fabric surfaces.
- **Key formal trait:** Japanese textile patterns tend toward clean, high-contrast, graphic forms. The line quality is crisp and the unit is visually self-contained.
- **Distinction:** these are textile patterns, not architectural geometry. Their construction logic is driven by weaving and dyeing, not compass and straightedge.

### European Gothic tracery
- **Construction:** derived from intersecting circles and pointed arches. Generates mullion patterns for windows, then adapted as surface ornament.
- **Preferred symmetries:** bilateral (in window designs), rotational (in rose windows).
- **Material:** carved stone, stained glass, manuscript borders, carved wood.
- **Behavior:** often contained within frames (window openings, panel borders) rather than extending as infinite fields.
- **Key formal trait:** pointed-arch geometry with branching subdivisions. Organic-looking but geometrically constructed.
- **Distinction:** Gothic tracery generates patterns through arch intersection, not star-polygon tiling. It looks vaguely similar to some Islamic geometric patterns but is constructed differently.

### Celtic interlace panels
- **Construction:** ribbon-based weaving logic. Strands pass over and under each other in continuous paths. Not tile-based.
- **Preferred symmetries:** bilateral within panels; translational in border applications.
- **Material:** manuscript illumination, carved stone crosses, metalwork.
- **Behavior:** typically contained within panels or borders, not infinite-field patterns.
- **Key formal trait:** the pattern is constructed from ribbons, not shapes. The interweaving is the primary visual event.
- **Distinction:** Celtic interlace is not tessellation. It is strand-weaving. The construction logic is fundamentally different from Islamic geometric or Japanese textile patterns.

### Slavic cross-stitch grids
- **Construction:** constrained by the grid of the fabric weave. Designs built on a rectilinear grid, one stitch per cell.
- **Preferred symmetries:** bilateral, translational. Rotational symmetry limited by the grid constraint.
- **Material:** linen, cotton; counted-thread embroidery.
- **Behavior:** band or border patterns; sometimes field-covering on garment yokes or towel ends.
- **Key formal trait:** angular, pixelated appearance dictated by the stitch grid. Curves are approximated as stepped diagonals.
- **Distinction:** the grid is physically imposed by the fabric, not chosen for aesthetic reasons. The visual character is a direct consequence of technique.

### South Asian jali screens
- **Construction:** perforated stone or lattice screens with geometric or vegetal patterns. Light passes through the openings.
- **Preferred symmetries:** bilateral, rotational; often hexagonal or radial.
- **Material:** carved sandstone (Mughal), carved marble, wooden lattice.
- **Behavior:** architectural — functions as light filter, privacy screen, and ventilation.
- **Key formal trait:** the pattern is defined by what is removed (openings), not what is added. Figure-ground relationship is literally physical.
- **Distinction:** jali is three-dimensional and functional. It is not surface decoration but architectural structure.

## Important symbolic differences

Geometric repeat patterns are often assumed to be culturally neutral — "just math." This is misleading.

- Islamic geometric patterns are sometimes interpreted as reflecting divine order or infinite creation — but this is one reading, not the sole purpose of every pattern
- Japanese textile patterns carry specific cultural associations (asanoha = growth/protection for children, seigaiha = calm seas) — these are conventional, not cosmic
- Mesoamerican stepped frets may carry cosmological associations in some contexts — but formal function as borders or field fills is equally important
- Celtic interlace is sometimes over-read as "eternity knots" — this is a modern popular interpretation, not necessarily the original meaning
- Slavic cross-stitch motifs are sometimes claimed to carry protective or calendrical meanings — evidence varies; many are primarily decorative or technique-driven

**Do not treat geometric patterns as meaning-free.** Also do not over-assign meaning.

---

## Common confusion risks

- **Islamic geometric ≠ all geometric** — Islamic tessellations follow specific construction rules. Not every geometric pattern is "Islamic-inspired."
- **Celtic interlace ≠ Islamic girih** — both involve interlacing, but the construction logic differs fundamentally (ribbon weaving vs. geometric grid).
- **Gothic tracery ≠ Islamic geometry** — pointed arches appear in both, but the systems are different.
- **Japanese asanoha ≠ Islamic hexagonal tiling** — both use hexagonal structures, but the aesthetic, material, and cultural context are different.
- **"Sacred geometry"** — a popular term that collapses multiple distinct geometric traditions into one vague category. Avoid it unless discussing the concept critically.
- **"Tribal pattern"** — a lazy term that erases the specificity of any geometric tradition it is applied to.

## Prompting / retrieval notes

- Specify tradition: "Islamic 10-fold star-polygon tessellation" or "Japanese asanoha textile repeat" or "Gothic window tracery" — not "geometric pattern"
- Specify construction type: "compass-and-straightedge star pattern," "woven ribbon interlace," "grid-constrained cross-stitch," "intersecting-arch tracery"
- Specify material: "cut-tile mosaic," "stencil-dyed cotton," "carved limestone window," "perforated marble screen"
- Specify behavior: "infinite-field repeat," "contained panel," "border band," "architectural screen"
- Avoid: "sacred geometry," "tribal pattern," "geometric ornament" without qualification
- For generative systems: specify the symmetry type (6-fold, 8-fold, bilateral, translational) and whether the pattern should tile infinitely or be contained within a frame
