# Cultural Respect and Accuracy

## Purpose

This file establishes how the repository handles cultural content without drifting into disrespect, flattening, or overclaiming.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Specificity is a form of respect

Naming a tradition correctly, distinguishing it from its neighbors, and describing its visual systems in concrete terms is more respectful than vague praise.

"Beautiful ancient patterns" is not respectful. It is lazy.

### 2. Do not invent symbolic meanings

If a motif's symbolic role is uncertain, broad, context-dependent, or debated, say so. Do not fabricate a tidy meaning to make the file feel more complete.

Invented certainty is a form of disrespect.

### 3. Do not flatten adjacent traditions

Maya and Aztec/Mexica are not interchangeable.
Japanese and Chinese decorative systems are not the same.
Islamic geometric ornament is not a synonym for all visual art from the Islamic world.
Russian folk motifs are not generic "European medieval."

Treating distinct traditions as interchangeable is a common and serious failure mode.

### 4. Avoid umbrella terms that erase specificity

Do not use:
- tribal
- exotic
- oriental
- primitive
- ethnic pattern
- ancient vibe

These words flatten. They signal that the writer has stopped thinking about what they are actually describing.

### 5. Scale claims to evidence

Not every motif has a well-documented symbolic history.
Not every region has the same depth of available scholarship.

When evidence is thin:
- say so
- reduce symbolic claims
- focus on formal visual analysis
- do not compensate by inventing meaning

---

## What accuracy looks like here

Accuracy in this repo means:

- naming the right culture or tradition
- describing visual traits concretely
- keeping symbolic claims proportionate to evidence
- flagging ambiguity and confusion risks
- distinguishing what is documented from what is speculative

---

## What disrespect looks like here

Disrespect in this repo tends to look like:

- collapsing distinct cultures into one aesthetic
- using sacred or culturally significant motifs as generic decoration
- inventing symbolic narratives
- applying "mystic wisdom" framing to living traditions
- lazy generalization

---

## Prompting and system implications

When generating prompts or structured data:

- always specify a culture or region rather than relying on broad labels
- avoid prompts that treat motifs as interchangeable stickers
- include anti-drift language where confusion is likely
- do not use "inspired by" as license to ignore specificity
