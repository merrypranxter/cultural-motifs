# Uncertainty and Evidence

## Purpose

This file explains how the repository handles uncertain, contested, or unevenly documented claims about motifs.

---

## The problem

Not all motifs have equally well-documented histories.
Not all symbolic interpretations are universally accepted.
Not all traditions have been studied with the same depth or care.

This unevenness is normal. The repo handles it by being honest about confidence levels rather than papering over gaps with invented certainty.

---

## How to signal uncertainty

Use phrases like:

- often associated with
- commonly used in
- one recurring interpretation is
- context-dependent
- not universal across all examples
- varies by period, place, and medium
- documented primarily in [specific context]
- speculative but recurrent in secondary sources

Avoid:

- always means
- universally represents
- the ancient meaning is
- this symbol has always been

---

## When to reduce symbolic claims

Reduce or omit symbolic claims when:

- the motif's meaning is debated among scholars
- the interpretation comes primarily from popular or secondary sources
- the symbolic reading is a modern projection onto older material
- the motif functions primarily as a formal or decorative element with no clear documented symbolic role

In these cases, focus on visual form, compositional behavior, and material context.

---

## When to flag contested interpretations

If a motif has competing interpretations:

- note the major readings
- note which contexts they apply to
- do not pick a winner unless the evidence strongly supports one
- do not merge competing readings into a single blended claim

---

## Evidence hierarchy

When available, prefer:

1. documented material evidence (surviving objects, inscriptions, period sources)
2. established scholarly consensus
3. well-sourced specialized references
4. cautiously used comparative evidence
5. secondary or popular interpretations (flagged as such)

Avoid treating popular retellings, New Age appropriations, or AI-generated summaries as primary evidence.

---

## What to do when you don't know

- say so
- narrow the scope
- focus on what is formally visible
- avoid compensating with invented narrative
- flag the gap for future expansion

A short, honest file is better than a long, confident, wrong one.
