# Motif Taxonomy Overview

## Purpose

This file provides the top-level classification system used across the repository to organize motifs by formal type. These categories are not cultures — they are shape-based and function-based groupings that help structure comparison, retrieval, and analysis.

---

## Major motif categories

### 1. Geometric motifs

Motifs defined primarily by geometric structure: grids, tessellations, star polygons, stepped frets, spirals, meanders, chevrons, concentric forms.

**Typical contexts:** architectural surfaces, tiled walls, textile repeats, manuscript borders, carved stone panels.

**Key formal traits:** high regularity, defined symmetry type, often tileable, often used as field or border elements.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Islamic star-polygon tessellations (8-fold, 10-fold, 12-fold)
- Greek key / meander borders
- Mesoamerican stepped fret (xicalcoliuhqui)
- Celtic spiral and triskele forms
- Slavic cross-stitch geometric repeats

---

### 2. Floral and vegetal motifs

Motifs based on plant forms: flowers, vines, leaves, trees, palmettes, lotuses, scrolling tendrils, arabesques.

**Typical contexts:** manuscript illumination, textile embroidery, carved and painted architectural surfaces, ceramic decoration.

**Key formal traits:** curvilinear, often symmetrical (bilateral or radial), frequently used in scrolling borders and field fills. Abstraction level varies widely — from naturalistic flowers to highly schematized vegetal scrolls.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Islamic vegetal arabesque (islimi)
- Egyptian lotus-and-papyrus borders
- Chinese peony, plum blossom, and cloud-scroll forms
- European acanthus scroll and rinceau
- South Asian lotus medallions and vine systems
- Japanese chrysanthemum and pine motifs

---

### 3. Fauna motifs

Motifs based on animal forms: birds, serpents, fish, lions, horses, hybrid beasts, insects.

**Typical contexts:** heraldry, textile design, architectural sculpture, ceramics, manuscript illumination, carved relief.

**Key formal traits:** varies from naturalistic to highly stylized. Often paired or mirrored. Frequently serves as emblem, guardian, or scene participant.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Egyptian falcon (Horus), scarab, cobra (uraeus)
- Chinese dragon (long), phoenix (fenghuang), qilin
- Mesoamerican feathered serpent (Quetzalcoatl imagery), jaguar
- Islamic paired birds, lion motifs (often under Persianate influence)
- European heraldic beasts (lion rampant, eagle displayed, griffin)
- Slavic firebird, double-headed eagle

---

### 4. Deity, guardian, and spirit motifs

Motifs depicting divine, protective, or supernatural figures: gods, guardian beasts, ancestor figures, threshold spirits.

**Typical contexts:** temple architecture, ritual objects, manuscript illumination, carved relief, ceremonial textiles.

**Key formal traits:** often frontal, often hierarchically scaled, frequently placed at thresholds or central positions. High degree of iconographic convention within traditions.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Egyptian deity figures (Anubis, Isis, Osiris) with codified attributes
- Hindu and Buddhist deity iconography with prescribed hand positions (mudras), attributes, and mounts (vahanas)
- Mesoamerican deity masks and composite figures
- Chinese/Japanese guardian lions (shishi/komainu)
- European Christian saints with codified attributes

**Caution:** Deity and guardian motifs are often the most culturally specific and iconographically regulated. Symbolic claims should be supported by documented convention, not guesswork.

---

### 5. Cosmology motifs

Motifs representing cosmic structures or celestial elements: suns, moons, stars, world trees, cosmic axes, layered heavens, creation diagrams.

**Typical contexts:** temple ceilings, ritual objects, manuscripts, architectural programs, textile designs.

**Key formal traits:** radial symmetry common, hierarchical layering, centering. Often used as organizing frames for larger compositions.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Egyptian sky-goddess (Nut) arching over compositions, solar disk forms
- Mesoamerican layered cosmos (upper, middle, lower worlds), sun stone imagery
- Hindu/Buddhist mandala structures
- Islamic geometric compositions read as cosmic order (interpretation varies)
- Chinese heaven-earth axis imagery

---

### 6. Architecture motifs

Motifs derived from or depicting architectural elements: arches, columns, niches, gates, stepped platforms, domes, vaults.

**Typical contexts:** architectural surfaces (self-referential), manuscript frames, textile designs mimicking architectural form, carved panel compositions.

**Key formal traits:** rectilinear or arched framing, hierarchical organization, often used as containment structures for other motifs.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Islamic muqarnas (stalactite vaulting), mihrab niche forms
- Egyptian temple pylons and column capitals
- Mesoamerican stepped pyramid profiles
- European Gothic tracery, Romanesque arcade motifs
- South Asian temple gopuram profiles, torana gate forms

---

### 7. Border, frame, and panel motifs

Motifs used primarily as edge containment, separation, or framing: meanders, guilloche, running scrolls, bead-and-reel, interlace bands, bracketed borders.

**Typical contexts:** manuscript margins, architectural moldings, textile edges, ceramic rims, floor borders.

**Key formal traits:** linear, repeating, typically one-directional or bilateral. Functions as visual containment rather than focal subject.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Greek egg-and-dart, bead-and-reel, meander
- Islamic geometric border bands
- Celtic interlace borders
- Chinese cloud-band and ruyi borders
- Mesoamerican stepped-fret borders
- European guilloche and rinceau borders

---

### 8. Textile and surface motifs

Motifs defined primarily by their behavior in woven, embroidered, printed, or stamped repeat systems.

**Typical contexts:** woven fabrics, embroidered garments, printed textiles, stamped ceramics, block-printed surfaces.

**Key formal traits:** repeat-unit logic, warp-weft constraints (for weaving), mirror and rotation symmetry, density governed by technique.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Islamic tilework repeat systems
- Indonesian batik patterns (parang, kawung, mega mendung)
- Japanese textile patterns (seigaiha, asanoha, shippo)
- Slavic cross-stitch and woven band patterns
- Andean textile repeat systems
- West African kente and adinkra patterns

---

### 9. Ceremonial object motifs

Motifs associated primarily with ritual or ceremonial objects: masks, vessels, weapons, crowns, altars, instruments.

**Typical contexts:** funerary objects, ritual vessels, crowns and regalia, temple furnishings, ceremonial weapons.

**Key formal traits:** highly codified within traditions, often combines multiple motif families (geometric + faunal + deity), placement on the object is meaningful.

**Examples by tradition:**
- Egyptian funerary masks, canopic jar motifs, pectoral jewelry
- Mesoamerican ritual masks, sacrificial vessels
- Chinese bronze vessel motifs (taotie, dragon, thunder pattern)
- European liturgical object decoration
- West African ceremonial mask and stool motifs

---

## How to use this taxonomy

- Use categories as a **first-pass filter**, not as a final destination.
- Every motif in a category should also be traceable to a specific culture or region.
- Categories may overlap (a lotus is both floral and cosmological in some contexts). When this happens, note the primary and secondary roles.
- Structured data files (JSON, CSV) should use these category names as stable keys.

---

## What this taxonomy does not do

This taxonomy does not:

- replace culture-specific analysis
- imply that all motifs in a category are interchangeable
- assign fixed symbolic meaning to formal categories
- cover every possible edge case

It is a structural convenience, not a theory of everything.
